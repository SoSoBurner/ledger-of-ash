# How Magic Changes by Polity

## Imperial Principalities broadly
Magic is never interpreted the same way everywhere. Noble privilege, temple authority, trade utility, and criminal law all compete to define what counts as acceptable use.

## Shelk
Shelk treats magic as culturally visible when it arrives through refinement, sanctioned craft, and ceremonial presentation. Enchanted textiles, house wards, cathedral rites, and archive-adjacent glyph scholarship fit the polity’s identity. Illicit magical use becomes especially dangerous when it threatens fashion designs, noble secrecy, civic order, or diplomatic hosting.

## Roaz
Roaz treats magic as a security and legal problem first. The key question is not whether magic works, but who is allowed to use it, who records it, and how quickly it can be suppressed. Detection, evidence handling, suppression fields, bastion engineering, and formal oversight matter more here than free magical expression.

## Mimolot
Mimolot reads magic through scholarship, archive custody, relic procedure, and instructional legitimacy. Magical authority often comes from the academy, curation systems, licensing, and text control rather than from purely personal charisma.

## Panim
Panim treats magic as sacred mediation, funerary process, and sanctified duty. Ritual order, proper custody, blessings, transitions, and the legality of sacred procedure matter more than experimentation.

## Sheresh Communes
Sheresh makes magic part of ordinary survival. Domes, containment systems, research chambers, power structures, and civic protection all rely on daily magical maintenance. Here, magical failure is not a private embarrassment; it can become a district-level crisis.

## Soreheim Alliance
Soreheim treats magic as rare, strategic, and supervised. Relics, wizard-advisors, and approved industrial or agricultural integrations may be respected, but unsanctioned experimentation is dangerous because it threatens production, survival, and communal order.

## Nomdara Caravan
Nomdara practice must adapt to multiple legal cultures. Portable wards, relic brokerage, ritual translation, and cross-jurisdiction caution matter more than assuming a single magical doctrine follows the caravan everywhere.

## Table-use shortcut
When the party enters a new polity, decide these five things before any magical scene:
1. what magic is publicly recognized here
2. what magic is tolerated but watched
3. what magic is automatically suspicious
4. who can certify or condemn a use
5. what non-combat consequence follows open casting
