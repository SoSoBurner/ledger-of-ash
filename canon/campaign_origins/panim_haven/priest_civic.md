# Priest — Civic Hand

You begin in Panim Haven, not as a tourist of its polity but as someone who already knows the
pressure of its thresholds. The air carries salt, incense, wax, cedar, linen, and low voices. Public
spaces feel watched by memory, accountancy, and doctrine. Daily life turns on records, mediation,
offering preparation, grief traffic, and the steady movement of family duty through sacred
administration. For a priest of the civic hand, this origin should feel native before it feels
dramatic. Your habits fit procedures, public expectations, and the small negotiations that keep a
locality from fraying in daylight. You hear pattern in the wrong phrase, weight in the wrong
blessing, and meaning in the places where doctrine and utility no longer quite agree. The first
disturbance should not arrive as a prophecy. It should feel local and survivable: a phrase repeated
with the wrong emotional logic, an object handled with too much private caution, a witness who
becomes calm too quickly, a small public correction that lands with the weight of suppression rather
than order.

The opening movement should stay anchored in Panim Haven while using Shelkopolis, Shirshal, and
Fairhaven only as adjacent pressure. Let rumors, freight, copied records, shrine traffic, or witness
behavior confirm that the same shape of unease exists one stop away, but never let those places
overwrite the origin. The player should first learn what wrongness looks like when filtered through
Panim Haven's own habits: divine balance obligations, processional strain and the cost of keeping
life ordinary under them.

Use Panim Haven Innkeeper as the first human anchor, then widen through workers, clerks, assistants,
patrol hands, shrine helpers, brokers, or record keepers. Let gossip belong to the people who
actually keep the place moving. They should speak about tensions in the language this locality
already uses: faith versus secrecy; grief versus cost; ritual dignity versus opportunistic trade.
Humor should come from competence, fatigue, and dry local standards, not parody.

If Rosalind is mentioned at all here, it should be by travelers and foreigners rather than locals: a
route captain from Shelkopolis, a scholar with fashionable ambitions, a merchant spouse talking
above their station for a moment too long. They do not accuse her of anything. They speak of her
with reverence, lust, admiration, or cultivated envy, as though House Shelk keeps somewhere in its
salons a woman who can steady embarrassment, sharpen courtship, and make private suffering look
beautiful. That rumor points toward Shelkopolis without making her a local actor.

The transition should come from an origin-rooted clue that refuses to stay contained: a copied
phrase in a record, a keepsake passed through ordinary hands, a witness naming a road mark, a shrine
worker comparing notes with a traveler. Use Shelkopolis and Shirshal as corroboration only, never as
a replacement for the origin, and let the trail tighten toward Fairhaven as the first place where
the pattern can be seen fully enough to follow.

## Transition to Stage II

In Panim Haven, the clue escapes through sacred and administrative channels: a grief record copied into another ledger, a ritual offering sent along the shrine network, a mediation document forwarded to a distant counterpart. Its first movement follows processional traffic, sacred duty, and shrine messengers rather than caravans. Only after shrines and administrators along the route echo the same unease does the trail tighten toward Fairhaven. Stage II begins there and runs through Fairhaven Chapel, Sunflower Market, The Gilded Plough Inn, Watcher’s Perch, Emberforge Outpost, and Plume’s End Outpost. However long or indirect your journey, these stations remain accessible; they form the spine of the story as you move from local misgiving to regional corruption.
