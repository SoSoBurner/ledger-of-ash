# Inquisitor — Civic Hand

You begin in Shirshal, not as a tourist of its polity but as someone who already knows the pressure
of its thresholds. People speak in lowered voices around cases. Ink, metal seals, burnt warding
materials, and rain-damp stone shape the atmosphere. The everyday tempo is built from casework,
witness traffic, evidence handling, magical anomaly review, and investigative rumor. For a
inquisitor of the civic hand, this origin should feel native before it feels dramatic. Your habits
fit procedures, public expectations, and the small negotiations that keep a locality from fraying in
daylight. You hear pattern in the wrong phrase, weight in the wrong blessing, and meaning in the
places where doctrine and utility no longer quite agree. The first disturbance should not arrive as
a prophecy. It should feel local and survivable: a phrase repeated with the wrong emotional logic,
an object handled with too much private caution, a witness who becomes calm too quickly, a small
public correction that lands with the weight of suppression rather than order.

The opening movement should stay anchored in Shirshal while using Panim Haven, Shelkopolis, and
Mimolot Academy only as adjacent pressure. Let rumors, freight, copied records, shrine traffic, or
witness behavior confirm that the same shape of unease exists one stop away, but never let those
places overwrite the origin. The player should first learn what wrongness looks like when filtered
through Shirshal's own habits: magical law violations, witness backlog and the cost of keeping life
ordinary under them.

Use Shirshal Innkeeper as the first human anchor, then widen through workers, clerks, assistants,
patrol hands, shrine helpers, brokers, or record keepers. Let gossip belong to the people who
actually keep the place moving. They should speak about tensions in the language this locality
already uses: truth versus convenience; safety versus secrecy; jurisdiction versus private power.
Humor should come from competence, fatigue, and dry local standards, not parody.

If Rosalind is mentioned at all here, it should be by travelers and foreigners rather than locals: a
route captain from Shelkopolis, a scholar with fashionable ambitions, a merchant spouse talking
above their station for a moment too long. They do not accuse her of anything. They speak of her
with reverence, lust, admiration, or cultivated envy, as though House Shelk keeps somewhere in its
salons a woman who can steady embarrassment, sharpen courtship, and make private suffering look
beautiful. That rumor points toward Shelkopolis without making her a local actor.

The transition should come from an origin-rooted clue that refuses to stay contained: a copied
phrase in a record, a keepsake passed through ordinary hands, a witness naming a road mark, a shrine
worker comparing notes with a traveler. Use Panim Haven and Shelkopolis as corroboration only, never
as a replacement for the origin, and let the trail tighten toward Fairhaven as the first place where
the pattern can be seen fully enough to follow.

## Transition to Stage II

In Shirshal, anomalous evidence moves through channels of casework and witness traffic: a copied statement sent to another magistrate, an evidence bundle couriered to a colleague, a witness escorted along the investigative circuit. The first leg of travel feels procedural and contested, expanding the jurisdiction from local inquiry to regional concern. As other investigators and shrines along the road confirm the same pattern, the trail converges on Fairhaven. There, Stage II unfolds through Fairhaven Chapel, Sunflower Market, The Gilded Plough Inn, Watcher’s Perch, Emberforge Outpost, and Plume’s End Outpost. However long or indirect your journey, these stations remain accessible and function as the spine of the story from local misgiving to regional corruption.
