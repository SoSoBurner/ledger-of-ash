# Necromancer — Frontier Hand

You begin in Mimolot Academy, not as a tourist of its polity but as someone who already knows the
pressure of its thresholds. Paper dust, lamp oil, polished desks, muttered debate, and courier
traffic carrying sealed packets. Instruction, copying, catalog review, tariff administration,
debate, and magical training dominate the rhythm of Mimolot life. For a necromancer of the frontier
hand, this origin should feel native before it feels dramatic. You read edge conditions faster than
officials do and notice the point where routine gives way to hazard, fatigue, or opportunism. You
hear pattern in the wrong phrase, weight in the wrong blessing, and meaning in the places where
doctrine and utility no longer quite agree. The first disturbance should not arrive as a prophecy.
It should feel local and survivable: a phrase repeated with the wrong emotional logic, an object
handled with too much private caution, a witness who becomes calm too quickly, a small public
correction that lands with the weight of suppression rather than order.

The opening movement should stay anchored in Mimolot Academy while using Fairhaven, and Shirshal
only as adjacent pressure. Let rumors, freight, copied records, shrine traffic, or witness behavior
confirm that the same shape of unease exists one stop away, but never let those places overwrite the
origin. The player should first learn what wrongness looks like when filtered through Mimolot
Academy's own habits: tariff tensions, faculty privilege disputes and the cost of keeping life
ordinary under them.

Use Mimolot Academy Innkeeper as the first human anchor, then widen through workers, clerks,
assistants, patrol hands, shrine helpers, brokers, or record keepers. Let gossip belong to the
people who actually keep the place moving. They should speak about tensions in the language this
locality already uses: knowledge versus access; scholarship versus privilege; precision versus
curiosity. Humor should come from competence, fatigue, and dry local standards, not parody.

If Rosalind is mentioned at all here, it should be by travelers and foreigners rather than locals: a
route captain from Shelkopolis, a scholar with fashionable ambitions, a merchant spouse talking
above their station for a moment too long. They do not accuse her of anything. They speak of her
with reverence, lust, admiration, or cultivated envy, as though House Shelk keeps somewhere in its
salons a woman who can steady embarrassment, sharpen courtship, and make private suffering look
beautiful. That rumor points toward Shelkopolis without making her a local actor.

The transition should come from an origin-rooted clue that refuses to stay contained: a copied
phrase in a record, a keepsake passed through ordinary hands, a witness naming a road mark, a shrine
worker comparing notes with a traveler. Use Fairhaven and Shirshal as corroboration only, never as a
replacement for the origin, and let the trail tighten toward Fairhaven as the first place where the
pattern can be seen fully enough to follow.

## Transition to Stage II

Within Mimolot Academy the clue that refuses to stay locked in a study or archive departs through scholarly dispatch: sealed packets to sister colleges, copied pages slipped into catalogues, couriers carrying a professor’s referral. Intellectual traffic becomes physical travel only once those records and couriers leave the academy and enter the shrine and market routes. As testimony from outside scholars aligns with your own, the trail narrows toward Fairhaven—the first place where theory meets lived consequence. There, Stage II proceeds through Fairhaven Chapel, Sunflower Market, The Gilded Plough Inn, Watcher’s Perch, Emberforge Outpost, and Plume’s End Outpost. However long or indirect your journey, these stations remain accessible and should anchor the story as you move from local misgiving to regional corruption.
