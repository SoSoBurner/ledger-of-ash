# Priest — Frontier Hand

You begin in Fairhaven, where the road out from Shelkopolis has already shed some of its polish and
shown more of its necessity. This town feeds, equips, and steadies lives, but it does so under the
pressure of glyph caves, shrine discipline, and the unnerving fact that the sky sometimes seems too
interested in what people are doing. Fairhaven Chapel, Sunflower Market, the Gilded Plough, and
Watcher's Perch should all feel like parts of one local body. The people here do not seek grandeur
from Felujitas. They seek continuance. That is what makes the wrongness dangerous. A comfort settles
too thoroughly. A mourning keepsake becomes emotionally necessary after blessing. A strained
companionship persists with spiritual weight but without warmth. The place should make the player
understand, before any lecture, that corruption survives because in the short term it helps.

Keep the action practical and close to the ground. Market exchanges go wrong. Travelers carry
delicate items under ordinary wrappings. Route hands compare shrine prayers and admit some of them
are making people steadier and stranger. Celestial creatures at Watcher's Perch feel less like
random danger and more like the land reacting to something the townsfolk still narrate as separate
incidents. Fairhaven is already the threshold of Stage II, so let the opener end not by leaving it
behind but by revealing how many of its ordinary places are connected.

Use The Gilded Plough Innkeeper as the first human anchor, then widen through workers, clerks,
assistants, patrol hands, shrine helpers, brokers, or record keepers. Let gossip belong to the
people who actually keep the place moving. They should speak about tensions in the language this
locality already uses: trade versus dignity; refinement versus necessity; public harmony versus
covert rivalry. Humor should come from competence, fatigue, and dry local standards, not parody.

If Rosalind is mentioned at all here, it should be by travelers and foreigners rather than locals: a
route captain from Shelkopolis, a scholar with fashionable ambitions, a merchant spouse talking
above their station for a moment too long. They do not accuse her of anything. They speak of her
with reverence, lust, admiration, or cultivated envy, as though House Shelk keeps somewhere in its
salons a woman who can steady embarrassment, sharpen courtship, and make private suffering look
beautiful. That rumor points toward Shelkopolis without making her a local actor.

Because you begin in Fairhaven, the transition is not a summons to arrive but a deepening of place.
End the opener by letting a local chapel helper, market runner, inn regular, or route hand connect
Fairhaven Chapel, Sunflower Market, Watcher's Perch, and the road out toward the outposts into a
single chain. The player should feel that Stage II begins because Fairhaven itself can no longer
pretend its comfort and damage are separate things.

## Stage II Continuation

Because you begin in Fairhaven, Stage II has already begun. Deepen your investigation through Fairhaven Chapel, Sunflower Market, The Gilded Plough Inn, and Watcher’s Perch. Then extend your road into Emberforge Outpost and Plume’s End Outpost, following the road network that threads through shrine waystations and travel halts. Each move from inn to hilltop to outpost brings you closer to understanding why the corruption persists.
