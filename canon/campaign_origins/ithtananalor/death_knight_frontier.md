# Death Knight — Frontier Hand

You begin in Ithtananalor, not as a tourist of its polity but as someone who already knows the
pressure of its thresholds. Air carries coal, oil, hot iron, damp paper, and ration kitchens.
Streets sound like boots, carts, hammers, whistles, and ordered commands. Daily life is shaped by
shifts, inspections, filings, quarry traffic, foundry schedules, and legal process. For a death
knight of the frontier hand, this origin should feel native before it feels dramatic. You read edge
conditions faster than officials do and notice the point where routine gives way to hazard, fatigue,
or opportunism. When tension rises, you instinctively scan for chokepoints, frightened witnesses,
and the first person who expects violence before anyone says it aloud. The first disturbance should
not arrive as a prophecy. It should feel local and survivable: a phrase repeated with the wrong
emotional logic, an object handled with too much private caution, a witness who becomes calm too
quickly, a small public correction that lands with the weight of suppression rather than order.

The opening movement should stay anchored in Ithtananalor while using Shelkopolis, Shirshal, and
Ironhold Quarry only as adjacent pressure. Let rumors, freight, copied records, shrine traffic, or
witness behavior confirm that the same shape of unease exists one stop away, but never let those
places overwrite the origin. The player should first learn what wrongness looks like when filtered
through Ithtananalor's own habits: illicit magic, smuggling and the cost of keeping life ordinary
under them.

Use Ithtananalor Innkeeper as the first human anchor, then widen through workers, clerks,
assistants, patrol hands, shrine helpers, brokers, or record keepers. Let gossip belong to the
people who actually keep the place moving. They should speak about tensions in the language this
locality already uses: law versus survival; security versus privacy; innovation versus containment.
Humor should come from competence, fatigue, and dry local standards, not parody.

If Rosalind is mentioned at all here, it should be by travelers and foreigners rather than locals: a
route captain from Shelkopolis, a scholar with fashionable ambitions, a merchant spouse talking
above their station for a moment too long. They do not accuse her of anything. They speak of her
with reverence, lust, admiration, or cultivated envy, as though House Shelk keeps somewhere in its
salons a woman who can steady embarrassment, sharpen courtship, and make private suffering look
beautiful. That rumor points toward Shelkopolis without making her a local actor.

The transition should come from an origin-rooted clue that refuses to stay contained: a copied
phrase in a record, a keepsake passed through ordinary hands, a witness naming a road mark, a shrine
worker comparing notes with a traveler. Use Shelkopolis and Shirshal as corroboration only, never as
a replacement for the origin, and let the trail tighten toward Fairhaven as the first place where
the pattern can be seen fully enough to follow.

## Transition to Stage II

In Ithtananalor, a record or object that refuses to be filed correctly slips out through official channels: inspection logs, legal filings, quarry shipments, and the controlled freight that runs between foundries. The first leg of travel is watched and scheduled under the eyes of inspectors and docket clerks, along marked industrial roads and labor convoys. Only after those convoys whisper the same wrongness beyond their jurisdiction does the trail tighten toward Fairhaven. Stage II then unfolds through Fairhaven Chapel, Sunflower Market, The Gilded Plough Inn, Watcher’s Perch, Emberforge Outpost, and Plume’s End Outpost. However long or indirect your journey, use those stations as the spine of the story from local misgiving to regional corruption.
