# Berserker — Civic Hand

You begin in Soreheim Proper, not as a tourist of its polity but as someone who already knows the
pressure of its thresholds. Air carries slag, grain mash, machine grease, smoke, wet stone, and
cooked communal meals. Sounds are horns, hammers, rolling freight, and shouted shift orders. Shifts,
quotas, repair cycles, hauling, planning, and ritualized contribution define ordinary life from
quarries to towers to farm zones. For a berserker of the civic hand, this origin should feel native
before it feels dramatic. Your habits fit procedures, public expectations, and the small
negotiations that keep a locality from fraying in daylight. When tension rises, you instinctively
scan for chokepoints, frightened witnesses, and the first person who expects violence before anyone
says it aloud. The first disturbance should not arrive as a prophecy. It should feel local and
survivable: a phrase repeated with the wrong emotional logic, an object handled with too much
private caution, a witness who becomes calm too quickly, a small public correction that lands with
the weight of suppression rather than order.

The opening movement should stay anchored in Soreheim Proper while using Harvest Circle, Sunspire
Haven, and Ironhold Quarry only as adjacent pressure. Let rumors, freight, copied records, shrine
traffic, or witness behavior confirm that the same shape of unease exists one stop away, but never
let those places overwrite the origin. The player should first learn what wrongness looks like when
filtered through Soreheim Proper's own habits: quota demands, political rivalry and the cost of
keeping life ordinary under them.

Use Soreheim Proper Innkeeper as the first human anchor, then widen through workers, clerks,
assistants, patrol hands, shrine helpers, brokers, or record keepers. Let gossip belong to the
people who actually keep the place moving. They should speak about tensions in the language this
locality already uses: ambition versus communal obligation; progress versus tradition; resource
demand versus local survival. Humor should come from competence, fatigue, and dry local standards,
not parody.

If Rosalind is mentioned at all here, it should be by travelers and foreigners rather than locals: a
route captain from Shelkopolis, a scholar with fashionable ambitions, a merchant spouse talking
above their station for a moment too long. They do not accuse her of anything. They speak of her
with reverence, lust, admiration, or cultivated envy, as though House Shelk keeps somewhere in its
salons a woman who can steady embarrassment, sharpen courtship, and make private suffering look
beautiful. That rumor points toward Shelkopolis without making her a local actor.

The transition should come from an origin-rooted clue that refuses to stay contained: a copied
phrase in a record, a keepsake passed through ordinary hands, a witness naming a road mark, a shrine
worker comparing notes with a traveler. Use Harvest Circle and Sunspire Haven as corroboration only,
never as a replacement for the origin, and let the trail tighten toward Fairhaven as the first place
where the pattern can be seen fully enough to follow.

## Transition to Stage II

In Soreheim Proper, the clue is carried outward by work itself: a freight load scheduled on a hauling route, a repair cycle note sent to a distant yard, a quota report that doubles as a warning. The first movement travels with industrial dispatches and labor convoys along long, harsh corridors—planned and procedural rather than spontaneous. Only when crews beyond your locality begin repeating the same wrongness does the trail converge on Fairhaven. Stage II then continues there through Fairhaven Chapel, Sunflower Market, The Gilded Plough Inn, Watcher’s Perch, Emberforge Outpost, and Plume’s End Outpost. However long or indirect your journey, use these stations as the accessible spine of the story as you move from local misgiving to regional corruption.
