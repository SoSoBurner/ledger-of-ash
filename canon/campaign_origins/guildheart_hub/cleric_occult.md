# Cleric — Occult Hand

You begin in Guildheart Hub, not as a tourist of its polity but as someone who already knows the
pressure of its thresholds. The air is full of chalk dust, ink, rope, cooking oil, hot metal, wet
crates, and argument. Streets feel loud, useful, and perpetually provisional. Union life is crowded
with arbitration, freight movement, guild disputes, workshops, changing obligations, and public
notice culture. For a cleric of the occult hand, this origin should feel native before it feels
dramatic. You feel the pressure behind words, rites, and objects, and you are harder to fool when
comfort arrives with the wrong shape. You hear pattern in the wrong phrase, weight in the wrong
blessing, and meaning in the places where doctrine and utility no longer quite agree. The first
disturbance should not arrive as a prophecy. It should feel local and survivable: a phrase repeated
with the wrong emotional logic, an object handled with too much private caution, a witness who
becomes calm too quickly, a small public correction that lands with the weight of suppression rather
than order.

The opening movement should stay anchored in Guildheart Hub while using Fairhaven, Shirshal,
Craftspire, and Unity Square only as adjacent pressure. Let rumors, freight, copied records, shrine
traffic, or witness behavior confirm that the same shape of unease exists one stop away, but never
let those places overwrite the origin. The player should first learn what wrongness looks like when
filtered through Guildheart Hub's own habits: legacy Mal claims, smuggling and the cost of keeping
life ordinary under them.

Use Guildheart Hub Innkeeper as the first human anchor, then widen through workers, clerks,
assistants, patrol hands, shrine helpers, brokers, or record keepers. Let gossip belong to the
people who actually keep the place moving. They should speak about tensions in the language this
locality already uses: trade versus dignity; arbitration versus coercion; successor legitimacy
versus expediency. Humor should come from competence, fatigue, and dry local standards, not parody.

If Rosalind is mentioned at all here, it should be by travelers and foreigners rather than locals: a
route captain from Shelkopolis, a scholar with fashionable ambitions, a merchant spouse talking
above their station for a moment too long. They do not accuse her of anything. They speak of her
with reverence, lust, admiration, or cultivated envy, as though House Shelk keeps somewhere in its
salons a woman who can steady embarrassment, sharpen courtship, and make private suffering look
beautiful. That rumor points toward Shelkopolis without making her a local actor.

The transition should come from an origin-rooted clue that refuses to stay contained: a copied
phrase in a record, a keepsake passed through ordinary hands, a witness naming a road mark, a shrine
worker comparing notes with a traveler. Use Fairhaven and Shirshal as corroboration only, never as a
replacement for the origin, and let the trail tighten toward Fairhaven as the first place where the
pattern can be seen fully enough to follow.

## Transition to Stage II

In Guildheart Hub, a clue that will not stay contained is pushed outward through the city’s own engines: a copied notice pinned among guild postings, a freight ledger with a phrase repeated too often, a broker whispering about a route mark. It travels at first with carts and couriers along freight corridors and arbitration traffic—busy and publicly argued rather than privately whispered. Only after union outposts and shrines along the route begin repeating the same wrongness does the trail narrow toward Fairhaven. There, Stage II runs through Fairhaven Chapel, Sunflower Market, The Gilded Plough Inn, Watcher’s Perch, Emberforge Outpost, and Plume’s End Outpost. However long or indirect your journey, these stations remain the accessible spine of the story, carrying you from local misgiving to regional corruption.
