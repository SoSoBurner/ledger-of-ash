# Tactician — Civic Hand

You begin in Shelkopolis, where arrival is already a social act. Roads are maintained like
arguments, traffic is ordered like ceremony, and even usefulness is expected to carry elegance. In
Verdant Row District and Ironspool Ward District, the city teaches itself through ordinary people:
artisans with thread under their nails, copyists with dry tongues, market staff who know which names
make everyone speak differently, and chapel workers who understand that renewal in Shelk is supposed
to mean dignity through restoration, not greatness through suffering. The first wrongness should
creep in by degrees. A blessing leaves someone too calm. A repaired object is suddenly impossible to
surrender. A younger attendant says a newer phrase of comfort that sounds right but settles too
deep. The city wants its surfaces kept beautiful even while something underneath begins to resist
being named.

Keep the early movement civic and tight. A witness runs from a lane toward the market. A shrine-
touched item appears where it should never have been sold. A library note remembers an earlier
incident in language that suddenly matters. Ironspool gives you the rougher organs of the same body,
where laborers, wagon repair, second-hand goods, and fear-charm buying show how close private fear
sits to public refinement. Build toward the House Garden Illumination as a social machine rather
than a one-night spectacle: overworked tailors, crowded inns, flower stock vanishing, carriages
polished, courtship sharpened, and everybody quietly trying to be seen by the right eyes for the
right reasons.

Use Amber Fountain Innkeeper as the first human anchor, then widen through workers, clerks,
assistants, patrol hands, shrine helpers, brokers, or record keepers. Let gossip belong to the
people who actually keep the place moving. They should speak about tensions in the language this
locality already uses: trade versus dignity; refinement versus necessity; public harmony versus
covert rivalry. Humor should come from competence, fatigue, and dry local standards, not parody.

Rosalind belongs here, but only after the city has been learned through ordinary hands. Let the
player first absorb Verdant Row, Ironspool, Silkweaver's Chapel, the library, the markets, and the
exquisite strain building toward the House Garden Illumination. Then let Rosalind enter in public
form at the Illumination or in its orbit: poised, gracious, emotionally exact, never infernal,
giving coherence where everyone else only gave fragments. She should feel like House Shelk's perfect
answer to embarrassment, grief, and courtly friction.

By the time the first chain is visible, the answer should not come from a noble or commander. Let an
artisan, clerk, chapel helper, copyist, or market-edge broker tell you that the same kind of
wrongness is being spoken of in Fairhaven, or that certain delicate objects are moving north along
the road under softer names. That is how the stage turns: not through proclamation, but through
local people admitting the pattern is no longer only a city problem.

## Transition to Stage II

The web of rumors and clues eventually drives you onto the road. Stage II begins when you leave Shelkopolis toward the shrine waystations and travel halts that dot the northern routes. From there the pattern pulls you to Fairhaven. Once there, the chain runs through Fairhaven Chapel, Sunflower Market, The Gilded Plough Inn, Watcher’s Perch, Emberforge Outpost, and Plume’s End Outpost before looping back through those same roads. Each stop reveals a new facet of the same corruption.
