# Berserker — Frontier Hand

You begin in Sunspire Haven, not as a tourist of its polity but as someone who already knows the
pressure of its thresholds. Air carries slag, grain mash, machine grease, smoke, wet stone, and
cooked communal meals. Sounds are horns, hammers, rolling freight, and shouted shift orders. Shifts,
quotas, repair cycles, hauling, planning, and ritualized contribution define ordinary life from
quarries to towers to farm zones. For a berserker of the frontier hand, this origin should feel
native before it feels dramatic. You read edge conditions faster than officials do and notice the
point where routine gives way to hazard, fatigue, or opportunism. When tension rises, you
instinctively scan for chokepoints, frightened witnesses, and the first person who expects violence
before anyone says it aloud. The first disturbance should not arrive as a prophecy. It should feel
local and survivable: a phrase repeated with the wrong emotional logic, an object handled with too
much private caution, a witness who becomes calm too quickly, a small public correction that lands
with the weight of suppression rather than order.

The opening movement should stay anchored in Sunspire Haven while using Harvest Circle, Soreheim
Proper, and Fairhaven only as adjacent pressure. Let rumors, freight, copied records, shrine
traffic, or witness behavior confirm that the same shape of unease exists one stop away, but never
let those places overwrite the origin. The player should first learn what wrongness looks like when
filtered through Sunspire Haven's own habits: predators, water system vulnerabilities and the cost
of keeping life ordinary under them.

Use Sunspire Haven Innkeeper as the first human anchor, then widen through workers, clerks,
assistants, patrol hands, shrine helpers, brokers, or record keepers. Let gossip belong to the
people who actually keep the place moving. They should speak about tensions in the language this
locality already uses: ambition versus communal obligation; progress versus tradition; resource
demand versus local survival. Humor should come from competence, fatigue, and dry local standards,
not parody.

If Rosalind is mentioned at all here, it should be by travelers and foreigners rather than locals: a
route captain from Shelkopolis, a scholar with fashionable ambitions, a merchant spouse talking
above their station for a moment too long. They do not accuse her of anything. They speak of her
with reverence, lust, admiration, or cultivated envy, as though House Shelk keeps somewhere in its
salons a woman who can steady embarrassment, sharpen courtship, and make private suffering look
beautiful. That rumor points toward Shelkopolis without making her a local actor.

The transition should come from an origin-rooted clue that refuses to stay contained: a copied
phrase in a record, a keepsake passed through ordinary hands, a witness naming a road mark, a shrine
worker comparing notes with a traveler. Use Harvest Circle and Soreheim Proper as corroboration
only, never as a replacement for the origin, and let the trail tighten toward Fairhaven as the first
place where the pattern can be seen fully enough to follow.

## Transition to Stage II

In Sunspire Haven, the clue departs via practical channels: a water-system report forwarded to another settlement, a patrol warning carried down the supply line, or a shrine helper comparing notes with travelers about predators and road damage. The first leg of the journey is guarded and pragmatic, moving along supply routes and patrol circuits that measure survival and infrastructure rather than ceremony. As warnings from further along the line match your own, the trail converges on Fairhaven. There, Stage II unfolds through Fairhaven Chapel, Sunflower Market, The Gilded Plough Inn, Watcher’s Perch, Emberforge Outpost, and Plume’s End Outpost. However long or indirect your journey, those stations remain accessible; use them as the spine of the story as you move from local misgiving to regional corruption.
