# Berserker — Occult Hand

You begin in Cosmoria, not as a tourist of its polity but as someone who already knows the pressure
of its thresholds. Salt, rope tar, wet wood, fish, ink, kelp, and hammered fittings define the
sensory field. Voices rise and fall with loading calls and weather alarms. Life turns on tides,
docking, repairs, recordkeeping, tax or treasury duty, and the constant trade between shore and
floating civic space. For a berserker of the occult hand, this origin should feel native before it
feels dramatic. You feel the pressure behind words, rites, and objects, and you are harder to fool
when comfort arrives with the wrong shape. When tension rises, you instinctively scan for
chokepoints, frightened witnesses, and the first person who expects violence before anyone says it
aloud. The first disturbance should not arrive as a prophecy. It should feel local and survivable: a
phrase repeated with the wrong emotional logic, an object handled with too much private caution, a
witness who becomes calm too quickly, a small public correction that lands with the weight of
suppression rather than order.

The opening movement should stay anchored in Cosmoria while using Guildheart Hub, and Fairhaven only
as adjacent pressure. Let rumors, freight, copied records, shrine traffic, or witness behavior
confirm that the same shape of unease exists one stop away, but never let those places overwrite the
origin. The player should first learn what wrongness looks like when filtered through Cosmoria's own
habits: storm weather, trade vulnerability and the cost of keeping life ordinary under them.

Use Cosmoria Innkeeper as the first human anchor, then widen through workers, clerks, assistants,
patrol hands, shrine helpers, brokers, or record keepers. Let gossip belong to the people who
actually keep the place moving. They should speak about tensions in the language this locality
already uses: trade versus safety; record truth versus profit; shore authority versus floating
custom. Humor should come from competence, fatigue, and dry local standards, not parody.

If Rosalind is mentioned at all here, it should be by travelers and foreigners rather than locals: a
route captain from Shelkopolis, a scholar with fashionable ambitions, a merchant spouse talking
above their station for a moment too long. They do not accuse her of anything. They speak of her
with reverence, lust, admiration, or cultivated envy, as though House Shelk keeps somewhere in its
salons a woman who can steady embarrassment, sharpen courtship, and make private suffering look
beautiful. That rumor points toward Shelkopolis without making her a local actor.

The transition should come from an origin-rooted clue that refuses to stay contained: a copied
phrase in a record, a keepsake passed through ordinary hands, a witness naming a road mark, a shrine
worker comparing notes with a traveler. Use Guildheart Hub and Fairhaven as corroboration only,
never as a replacement for the origin, and let the trail tighten toward Fairhaven as the first place
where the pattern can be seen fully enough to follow.

## Transition to Stage II

An origin clue that refuses to stay contained in Cosmoria moves first across water: a copied phrase in a harbor log, a keepsake passed between floating civic space and shore, a shrine worker comparing notes with a route captain. Only after it crosses from tide to land does it join the inland road network, trading salt spray for shrine waystations and travel halts. The trail narrows toward Fairhaven, where the full pattern finally takes form. Once there, Stage II unfolds through Fairhaven Chapel, Sunflower Market, The Gilded Plough Inn, Watcher’s Perch, Emberforge Outpost, and Plume’s End Outpost. However long or indirect your route, these stations remain accessible; use them as the spine of the story as you move from local misgiving to regional corruption.
