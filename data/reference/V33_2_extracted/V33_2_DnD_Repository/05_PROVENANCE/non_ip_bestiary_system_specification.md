# Bestiary System Specification

## 1. System Overview

The bestiary of this setting is not a generic monster list and must not be treated as a detached encounter catalog. It is a **world-native ecological, metaphysical, infrastructural, and social organism registry** operating inside a damaged but stabilized planetary system. Creatures in this world are not merely “things to fight.” They are expressions of climate, route pressure, divine residue, local adaptation, contamination exposure, predatory logic, ritual history, planar adjacency, labor systems, and the specific survival technologies of each polity.

A valid bestiary entry in this setting therefore cannot stop at anatomy or combat capability. It must specify the creature’s:

- ecological niche
- reproductive or persistence model
- relation to routes and settlements
- sensitivity to law and magical infrastructure
- contamination profile
- institutional handling status
- symbolic meaning in local culture
- salvage value
- anti-threat procedures
- worldbuilding implications if present in stable numbers

### 1.1 Primary Function
The bestiary framework exists to classify all non-human and non-civil human-adjacent lifeforms, entities, constructs, emergent hazards, anomalous organisms, and quasi-living manifestations that materially affect the world through one or more of the following:

- predation
- scavenging
- disease or contamination spread
- transportation disruption
- labor integration
- domestication
- spiritual significance
- relic interaction
- site corruption
- route pressure
- weather adaptation
- military utility
- social fear
- myth production
- local legitimacy
- sacred or taboo function

### 1.2 System Model
The bestiary operates as a **multi-axis organism and entity classification architecture** composed of the following layers:

- **Biological Layer**: body plan, reproductive model, metabolism, sensory profile, and habitat fit
- **Ecological Layer**: trophic role, migration behavior, competition pattern, scavenger interactions, and settlement pressure
- **Metaphysical Layer**: relation to magic, contamination, divine residue, planar bleed, or ritual susceptibility
- **Behavioral Layer**: threat style, intelligence profile, learning behavior, territoriality, sociality, and adaptability
- **Civilizational Layer**: how communities regulate, hunt, fear, use, worship, or suppress the creature
- **Institutional Layer**: what offices, guilds, wardens, engineers, or chapels are responsible for response
- **Salvage Layer**: usable remains, dangerous remains, relic potential, contamination handling, and carcass rights
- **Narrative Layer**: what the creature means to the people who live around it, including folklore, class dynamics, law, and memory

### 1.3 System Identity
This bestiary must always be treated as:

- regionally specific
- ecologically consequential
- politically entangled
- partially magical but not uniformly magical
- sensitive to climate and route conditions
- shaped by post-rupture world logic
- inseparable from contamination, infrastructure, and settlement adaptation
- useful for design of scenes, travel, law, and economy, not only combat

### 1.4 Design Assumption
The Material Planet is not a clean natural world. Creature life in this setting unfolds inside a reality already shaped by:

- divine fragmentation
- elemental planetary stabilization
- local magical pressure
- doom-scar inheritance
- artificial domes
- floating settlements
- fortified corridors
- ritual sites
- route dependence
- institutional intervention

Accordingly, the bestiary must include not only ordinary fauna and apex predators, but also:

- ritual-altered animals
- contamination-adapted organisms
- location-bound hazards
- magical infrastructure symbiotes
- false lifeforms produced by glyph or relic influence
- planar bleed entities
- sacred enforcers
- managed threat species
- semi-domestic war-beasts
- salvage organisms that grow around death, metal, heat, or magical residue

## 2. Foundational Principles

### 2.1 Life Baseline
Life in this world is not divided cleanly into mundane and magical. Many species exist on a gradient between biological adaptation and metaphysical influence.

A valid ecological baseline must therefore account for:

- ordinary evolutionary pressure
- magical environment pressure
- exposure to contamination
- settlement engineering
- ritual and agricultural shaping
- inherited regional anomaly patterns
- interaction with non-material planes or residues

### 2.2 Creature Definition
A creature, for purposes of this system, is any persistent or semi-persistent non-human agent or organism whose existence has repeatable behavioral and environmental consequences.

This includes:

- natural animals
- magical beasts
- sacred agents
- contamination-warped organisms
- quasi-elemental fauna
- large predatory anomalies
- semi-sentient wild entities
- scavenging horrors
- social species outside formal humanoid civilization
- route hazards with organism-like patterns
- site-bound emergent things that reproduce, recur, or feed

### 2.3 Ecological Cost
No creature exists in isolation. Every meaningful species imposes one or more costs on the world:

- food demand
- prey pressure
- infrastructure strain
- labor burden
- defensive expense
- magical maintenance cost
- migration disruption
- social fear
- salvage opportunity
- disease propagation
- law enforcement overhead
- ritual complication
- trade rerouting
- communal adaptation pressure

### 2.4 Adaptation Principle
The stranger the creature, the more specifically it must fit some pressure of the setting.

Creatures do not exist simply because fantasy worlds “have monsters.” Every species or entity class should answer one or more of the following:

- What climate or magical pressure produced it?
- What niche does it occupy?
- What human behavior does it punish?
- What institution exists because of it?
- What route changes around it?
- What material economy uses its remains?
- What social memory persists because of it?
- What settlement technology prevents it from winning?

### 2.5 Persistence Principle
A creature must persist through one or more models:

- biological reproduction
- spore or brood dispersal
- magical self-renewal
- contamination re-emergence
- relic or site spawning
- ritual summoning
- seasonal surfacing
- divine or sacred manifestation under repeatable conditions
- parasitic continuation through hosts or zones

### 2.6 Classification Principle
The bestiary is not organized only by threat. It is organized by **world function**. A low-threat species may be more important than a great predator if it:

- reveals contamination
- damages stored grain
- undermines levees
- weakens wards
- disrupts reserve accounting
- alters migration of larger animals
- marks sacred thresholds
- becomes necessary in textile, alchemical, or medicinal economies

## 3. Eligibility for Inclusion

An organism, entity, or repeating hazard pattern should be included in the bestiary if it satisfies at least one of the following:

### 3.1 Ecological Relevance
It has measurable impact on:

- food chains
- migration
- weather adaptation
- settlement planning
- labor routines
- agricultural outcomes
- route viability
- carcass or salvage systems

### 3.2 Civilizational Relevance
It changes how societies behave through:

- fear
- worship
- law
- domestication
- trade
- military doctrine
- infrastructure maintenance
- property or carcass rights
- noble obligations
- local taboo

### 3.3 Metaphysical Relevance
It interacts meaningfully with:

- magic
- contamination
- sacred practice
- planar boundaries
- relic fields
- glyph activity
- domes
- anti-magic engineering
- sanctified architecture

### 3.4 Narrative Relevance
It produces recurring situations such as:

- corridor tension
- hunt obligations
- disappearance rumors
- shrine controversy
- falsified sightings
- salvage disputes
- house prestige hunts
- border incidents
- quarantine decisions
- public panic

### 3.5 Institutional Relevance
There is a known office, stewardship role, registry, management lane, or response protocol dedicated to it.

## 4. Classification Architecture

All bestiary entries are to be classified across multiple parallel axes rather than a single species label.

### 4.1 Origin Category
A creature must be assigned an origin class.

#### 4.1.1 Natural Fauna
Species produced primarily through ordinary biological evolution with limited magical deviation.

#### 4.1.2 Magically Adapted Fauna
Biological species shaped by long-term exposure to magical pressure, unusual climates, ritual practice, or regional anomaly fields.

#### 4.1.3 Contamination-Warped Fauna
Creatures altered by persistent environmental corruption, polar magical stress, glyph damage, seepage, or doom-scar influence.

#### 4.1.4 Sacred or Divine-Adjacency Entities
Beings tied to chapel sites, sacred geographies, doctrinal enforcement, omen-bearing duties, or divine residue.

#### 4.1.5 Elemental-Influenced Organisms
Creatures whose life-cycle or anatomy is strongly shaped by fire, ash, stone, storm, ice, or other large-scale environmental intensities.

#### 4.1.6 Constructed or Engineered Organisms
Artificially bred, alchemically shaped, ritual-grown, or institutionally maintained living or quasi-living beings.

#### 4.1.7 Planar or Threshold Entities
Beings that emerge from, overlap with, or partially inhabit adjacent metaphysical layers.

#### 4.1.8 Recurrent Hazard Lifeforms
Entities that blur the line between organism and environmental hazard through repeatable feeding, nesting, or manifesting behavior.

## 11. Threat and Encounter Architecture

## 11.1 Encounter Bands
Creatures should be classified for scene use across four broad bands:

### 11.1.1 Ambient
Heard, seen, signaled, or implied; creates tension without immediate attack.

### 11.1.2 Procedural Hazard
Requires correct travel, camp, handling, or ritual procedure to avoid consequence.

### 11.1.3 Tactical Adversary
Creates a contained threat event with meaningful positioning and response choices.

### 11.1.4 Regional Pressure Event
A creature or swarm whose presence changes law, labor, or travel patterns for an entire district or corridor.

- who has authority to kill or contain it
- what parts may be salvaged
- what parts must be burned, buried, sanctified, or sealed
- whether witness documentation is required
- whether the site must be quarantined afterward

- ritual-useful

- contamination-risk only

- cold

- sanctified wrapping

- its bones reinforce ceremonial tools
- its feathers carry status symbolism
- its carcass proves a noble fulfilled an obligation
- its remains justify a house claim over cleared ground

## 19. Balancing Constraints

## 19.1 Ecological Balance
A creature should not exist in large numbers unless:
- it has sufficient prey or fuel
- the settlement adaptation makes sense
- a management institution exists
- its reproduction supports that density
- its impact has visibly changed the world

## 19.2 Threat Balance
Not every species must be deadly. Variety should include:
- nuisance species
- omen species
- salvage species
- prestige species
- transport species
- awe species
- procedural hazard species
- apex event species

## 19.3 Worldbuilding Balance
A creature that is too strong for its environment must trigger one of:
- heavy culling infrastructure
- abandonment of the region
- sacred taboo zones
- state monopoly response

- alternative settlement architecture

## 19.4 Magical Balance
A magically empowered creature must specify:

## 20. Design Summary

### 20.1 Core Definition
A non-IP bestiary for this setting is a structured registry of the world’s organisms, anomalous lifeforms, sacred entities, engineered beasts, contamination fauna, and recurring ecological hazards, each defined through biology, behavior, ecology, metaphysics, infrastructure impact, and social meaning.

### 20.2 System Identity
This is a **world-embedded, ecology-first, route-aware, institution-sensitive bestiary architecture** optimized for:

- encounter design
- travel realism
- legal and political entanglement
- vivid regional identity
- salvage economies
- contamination management
- sacred and folkloric ambiguity
- believable infrastructure adaptation

### 20.3 Final Technical Summary
A creature in this setting is not fully specified until the designer can answer:

- What pressure produced it?
- What niche does it fill?
- What signs precede it?
- What human systems exist because of it?
- What does it do to routes, settlements, and law?
- What part of it is useful, dangerous, sacred, or taboo?
- What happens if its numbers rise or collapse?
- What institution claims authority over it?
- What does the surrounding culture say it means?

### 20.4 Setting-Specific Summary
On this world, beasts are not background decoration. They are frontier policy, dome pressure, maritime diplomacy, sacred anxiety, salvage law, family prestige, labor discipline, and the visible proof that the Material Planet is still being contested by climate, magic, contamination, and memory.

### 20.5 Use Standard
When creating any new bestiary entry for this setting, evaluate it against the following checklist:

- Which region or corridor produces it?
- Is it natural, adapted, warped, sacred, or engineered?
- What does it eat, fear, and follow?
- What environmental signs does it leave?
- How does it affect routes, labor, and law?
- Who hunts, manages, worships, or hides it?
- What remains are valuable or dangerous?
- What part of local culture has formed around it?
- What larger world system becomes visible through this creature?
