# Magical System Specification

## 1. System Overview

The magical system of this setting is not a generic, universalized “spellcasting” model. It is a **world-native, cosmologically anchored, socially mediated, jurisdiction-sensitive reality intervention framework** operating inside a damaged but stabilized creation. Magic is a first-order structural force of the world, shaped by the self-division of the Primordial Unity, the divine shards that emerged thereafter, the rupture event known as Doomsday, the stabilizing role of the elemental planetary bodies, and the continuing pressure exerted by adjacent cosmological layers such as the Astral Sky, the Ethereal Plane, Umbra, Mother Storm, Limbo, and the Infinite Staircase.

Magic therefore cannot be modeled as a simple personal talent. It must be modeled as a **distributed interaction between world substrate, authorized pattern, user discipline, local law, cultic interpretation, material channels, and environmental pressure**.

In operational terms, magic is the controlled imposition of non-natural outcomes onto the Material Planet through procedures, authorities, structures, and anomalies capable of overriding ordinary physical, biological, social, or metaphysical limits.

### 1.1 Primary Function
Magic enables a qualified entity, institution, relic, site, lineage, or divine interface to alter one or more of the following beyond conventional natural means:

- matter
- heat
- light
- force
- life-processes
- cognition
- memory
- spirit-state
- perception
- distance
- motion
- thresholds and boundaries
- concealment and revelation
- oath-binding and ritual legitimacy
- continuity between planes or layers
- material integrity
- symbolic value and enchantment function

### 1.2 System Model
Magic operates as an **exception-based reality override architecture** with the following core layers:

- **Cosmological Substrate Layer**: the world’s baseline metaphysical condition, including divine shard inheritance, astral partitioning, doom-scar instability, elemental stabilization, and adjacency to non-material layers
- **Access Layer**: determines what effects a user, relic, institution, bloodline, rite, or office is capable of invoking
- **Authorization Layer**: determines whether a use is legitimate, recognized, tolerated, illicit, sacred, or state-controlled
- **Resource Layer**: determines how many effects can be invoked and at what intensity
- **Execution Layer**: defines how an effect is initiated and shaped
- **Validation Layer**: checks whether jurisdictional, ritual, material, bodily, spatial, and symbolic conditions are met
- **Resolution Layer**: determines whether the effect succeeds, is resisted, degraded, redirected, or fails
- **Persistence Layer**: governs how long the effect remains active and what maintains it
- **Interference Layer**: governs suppression, counteraction, contamination, nullification, and legal response
- **World Consequence Layer**: governs what magical use changes in memory, legitimacy, infrastructure, politics, fear, and future environmental stability

### 1.3 System Identity
This setting’s magic must always be treated as:

- regionally variable
- institutionally mediated
- cosmology-dependent
- spiritually and politically charged
- occasionally dangerous even when successful
- never globally lawful by default
- never culturally interpreted in only one way
- inseparable from material history and world trauma

### 1.4 Operating Assumption
The Material Planet is not a pristine magical environment. It is a **post-rupture stabilized world**. Magic therefore operates in a reality that has already been broken, sealed, partitioned, and continuously held together. This means:

- some magic is easier in some places because reality is thinner there
- some magic is harder in some places because reality is actively reinforced there
- some effects that seem “normal” in one polity are criminal or heretical in another
- some regions develop magical infrastructure defensively rather than artistically
- some traditions interpret magic as scholarship, some as devotion, some as inheritance, some as contamination management, and some as state security risk

## 2. Foundational Principles

### 2.1 Reality Baseline
Reality has a default stable state governed by natural law, planetary stabilization, divine residue, and scar-managed continuity. That baseline is not wholly mundane. It already includes:

- the Material Planet’s position inside the bisected Astral Sky
- the continuing influence of the Planet of Fire, Ocean, Wind, and Rock
- axial instability inherited from doom scars
- mirrored or echo-adjacent existence through the Ethereal Plane
- shadow and density distortions associated with Umbra
- storm-pressure phenomena associated with Mother Storm
- sealed or dangerous instability associated with Limbo

This means baseline reality is **stable enough for life, unstable enough for magic, and structured enough for magical regulation to matter**.

### 2.2 Magic Definition
Magic is the process by which an authorized or capable actor imposes an artificial pattern onto reality, temporarily or permanently replacing natural outcomes with desired outcomes by exploiting one or more of the following:

- divine residue
- inherited alignment to world-force
- ritual geometry
- symbolic resonance
- sanctioned doctrinal form
- trained cognitive structuring
- material anchor behavior
- relic mediation
- environmental pressure differential
- anomaly exposure
- controlled planar adjacency

### 2.3 Operational Cost
All non-trivial magical effects consume one or more of the following:

- internal vitality
- trained mental bandwidth
- devotional or doctrinal alignment
- prepared symbolic structure
- patronal or divine authorization
- site-based charge
- stored ritual mass
- crafted focus stability
- environmental permissiveness
- rare materials
- legal cover
- social legitimacy
- time
- bodily risk
- contamination tolerance

### 2.4 Scaling Principle
The greater the deviation from natural law, the greater the burden imposed in one or more of the following areas:

- energy cost
- symbolic complexity
- casting duration
- required training
- institutional clearance
- material specificity
- location dependence
- contamination exposure
- visibility to hostile authorities
- cognitive strain
- persistence instability
- resistance threshold
- backlash potential
- downstream legal or theological consequence

### 2.5 Conservation Principle
Magic does not create consequence-free miracles. It reallocates pressure. An effect that produces order somewhere may:

- draw heat, force, or entropy from elsewhere
- invite planar attention
- weaken a ward
- leave residue
- create traceability
- distort memory
- alter sanctity
- burden a site custodian
- escalate local enforcement

### 2.6 Legibility Principle
In this world, one of the most important variables is whether an act of magic is **legible**. Legibility determines whether the act can be:

- named
- classified
- sanctioned
- taxed
- archived
- worshipfully interpreted
- denounced
- prosecuted
- repeated safely
- countered effectively

An illegible magical act is often more feared than a powerful one because nobody agrees what category of danger it belongs to.

## 3. User Eligibility

An entity may access magic if it possesses a valid magical interface mechanism and sufficient world compatibility to shape effects without immediate collapse or nullification.

### 3.1 Qualified User Types
Examples of valid magical access sources include:

- trained arcane cognition
- inherited magical physiology
- divine shard adjacency
- spiritual attunement
- oath-bound vocation
- pact-based authorization
- temple-mediated liturgy
- runic or symbolic engineering
- guild-certified enchantment practice
- artifact-mediated access
- relic custodianship
- anomaly exposure with survivable stabilization
- office-based ritual authority
- locality-bound custodial role
- state-controlled technical clearance

### 3.2 Access Requirements
A user must have:

- a valid magical source
- sufficient control to shape an effect
- the ability to satisfy execution conditions
- available energy or other required cost medium
- enough symbolic literacy to distinguish valid from invalid form
- bodily compatibility with the required mode of use
- access to a stable or tolerable ritual environment
- local legitimacy or willingness to absorb illegitimacy consequences
- immunity, resilience, or procedural safeguards against relevant magical blowback

### 3.3 Ineligible Users
An entity is functionally ineligible if it lacks one or more of the following:

- metaphysical compatibility
- ritual literacy
- stable will
- symbolic memory
- channel integrity
- material access
- permission from required divine, state, or office structure
- survival threshold for contamination or backlash

### 3.4 Conditional Eligibility
Some users are eligible only under specific conditions, such as:

- inside a chapel, academy, or sanctioned chamber
- while carrying designated office tokens
- while using guild-registered implements
- during permitted observance cycles
- in the presence of authorized witnesses
- on designated ley-quiet or ley-open days
- while the local reality pressure remains below emergency threshold
- while not crossing into prohibited jurisdictions such as Roaz’s unsanctioned practice regimes

### 3.5 Institutional Eligibility
In this setting, institutions can act as effective magical users. A chapel, academy, bastion, archive, warded dome, relic vault, or fortification complex may be able to:

- hold charge
- authorize rituals
- filter users
- augment effect precision
- suppress illicit working
- register magical signatures
- absorb excess strain
- route power through approved conduits

This means magic is not only personal. It is also **architectural and administrative**.

## 4. Effect Classification

All magical operations are divided into two primary categories, but in this setting those categories must also be interpreted socially and cosmologically.

### 4.1 Minor Effects
Low-complexity, low-cost, repeatable magical expressions.

#### Properties
- negligible or self-replenishing cost
- low immediate reality strain
- short execution time
- narrow symbolic footprint
- limited scale and impact
- suitable for repeated use
- more likely to be normalized in artisanal, civic, or scholastic environments
- still potentially illegal if performed in the wrong place or manner

#### Functional Examples
- thread-lighting for textile work
- heat-tuning for forge or dye processes
- light shaping for script or archive reading
- object-cleaning, drying, or sorting
- harmless illusion work
- localized sensory sharpening
- seal recognition
- minor ward contact checks
- candle, loom, needle, or glass-work assistance
- voice-carry within controlled distance
- low-grade concealment or presentation refinement

#### Social Examples
In one locality, such practices may be ordinary craft adjuncts. In another, the same behaviors may count as unauthorized interference in sacred or civic spaces.

### 4.2 Major Effects
Structured, resource-consuming magical operations with meaningful impact on body, mind, environment, law, infrastructure, or cosmological permeability.

#### Properties
- consumes finite magical resources
- generates stronger trace
- carries non-trivial social or legal risk
- increased execution requirements
- broader or deeper outcome range
- may require sanctioned space or witness
- may require resistance checks
- may require active maintenance
- may attract countermeasure response
- may alter local legitimacy, memory, or fear conditions even when successful

#### Functional Examples
- battlefield force release
- surgical healing or restoration
- curse breaking
- mind influence
- oath-binding
- barrier erection
- relic activation
- ward lattice installation
- summoning or calling
- controlled planar bridging
- sanctification
- contamination sealing
- architectural enchantment
- anti-magic suppression fields
- resurrection-class intervention
- weather-scale or zone-scale manipulation

### 4.3 Civic Classification Overlay
All magical effects may additionally be classified as:

- civic
- artisanal
- scholastic
- liturgical
- military
- clandestine
- custodial
- relic-mediated
- prohibited
- contaminated
- emergency-authorized
- noble-licensed
- state-monopolized

### 4.4 Site Sensitivity Overlay
An effect category may escalate in legal seriousness when used inside:

- libraries
- academies
- sacred thresholds
- public command structures
- relic rooms
- fortifications
- tariff-protected archives
- domed contamination zones
- textile enchantment houses
- ritual chapels

## 5. Resource Architecture

## 5.1 Primary Resource Model
Magic uses a **ranked expenditure framework** but does not reduce cleanly to a single fuel. Instead, magical output may be financed by several partially overlapping resource classes:

- internal reserve
- ritual preparation mass
- devotional sanction
- relic charge
- site charge
- office warrant
- environmental permissiveness
- group-casting load distribution
- sacrificial or consumed material medium

Each user or institution has access to a set of energy allocations partitioned by **effect tier**, but those tiers are filtered through setting-native interpretation.

### 5.2 Tier Characteristics
Higher tiers represent greater:

- energy density
- symbolic intricacy
- coercive authority over reality
- strain on bodily and mental structure
- visibility to magical observers
- need for institutional buffering
- risk of jurisdictional scrutiny
- likelihood of planar or spiritual consequence

Each tier supports:
- activation of effects assigned to that tier
- activation of lower-tier effects through overcasting
- enhanced output where scaling rules exist
- broader shape or duration if the effect template allows
- deeper inscription into the world if the user can survive the cost

### 5.3 Resource Classes

#### 5.3.1 Personal Reserve
The user’s internal magical endurance, often limited by training, lineage, health, and repeated stress.

#### 5.3.2 Prepared Structure
Patterns previously encoded through memorization, inscription, prayer-cycle, geometry, loom-work, or archive discipline.

#### 5.3.3 Site Reserve
Power held by a place, such as a chapel, academy, dome, bastion, forge, archive, or ancestral hall.

#### 5.3.4 Relic Reserve
Stored or quasi-living charge inside an item, shard, vestment, seal, weapon, lens, spindle, glasswork, or devotional object.

#### 5.3.5 Collective Reserve
Distributed load across multiple ritual participants.

#### 5.3.6 Authority Reserve
The effective capacity granted by an office, law, temple, noble house, or state apparatus to make an act “hold” in the world.

### 5.4 Overcasting Rule
A higher-tier resource unit may be used to activate a lower-tier effect only if the effect definition permits substitution.

Possible scaling outcomes:
- increased intensity
- expanded target count
- wider area
- longer duration
- greater restoration
- improved resistance threshold
- more stable persistence
- reduced component fragility
- enhanced anti-interference resilience
- more durable environmental inscription

### 5.5 Recovery Model
Magical resources recover according to one or more recovery cycles:

- sleep and bodily reset
- meditation or disciplined recollection
- liturgical observance
- shrine or temple attendance
- ritual reset at fixed temporal intervals
- environmental recharge
- relic reattunement
- license renewals or formal reauthorization
- site resonance replenishment
- group rite rebalancing
- seasonal planetary influence shifts
- polarity or cosmological pressure windows

### 5.6 Resource Failure
If adequate resources are unavailable, the effect cannot be executed unless a special override rule exists.

Possible override consequences:
- physical collapse
- loss of consciousness
- memory shear
- voice damage
- tremor or motor degradation
- contamination uptake
- unwanted planar leakage
- spiritual branding
- legal trace amplification
- relic fracture
- chamber failure
- partial manifestation
- target misassignment
- corruption of nearby symbolic systems

### 5.7 Resource Quality
Not all magical fuel of similar magnitude is interchangeable. Power may differ in:

- purity
- doctrinal compatibility
- volatility
- contamination level
- local legitimacy
- planar signature
- divine resonance
- element alignment
- emotional imprint
- archival reproducibility

### 5.8 Resource Scarcity as Politics
Because some effects require sanctioned chambers, registered implements, tariff-protected texts, or house-controlled knowledge, access to magic is partly a **class and infrastructure question**, not merely a personal talent question.

## 6. Access Models

The system supports multiple effect-access architectures. In this setting, these architectures are not just class flavors; they are different **civilizational relationships to power**.

### 6.1 Fixed-Known Model
User permanently knows a limited set of magical effects.

#### Properties
- low flexibility
- high execution familiarity
- reduced preparation burden
- strong specialization
- strong lineage or apprenticeship identity
- often associated with house traditions, inherited forms, or trade-hardened working knowledge

#### Typical Setting Contexts
- family enchantment craft
- field-tested practical mages
- shrine functionaries
- relic custodians
- battlefield specialists
- contamination technicians

### 6.2 Prepared Loadout Model
User has access to a larger effect catalog but may only ready a limited subset for active use during a given cycle.

#### Properties
- high strategic flexibility
- dependence on planning
- increased logistical complexity
- strong archive and library dependence
- high value of foreknowledge
- more sensitive to tariffs, record access, and institutional control

#### Typical Setting Contexts
- academy-trained scholars
- sanctioned ritualists
- noble house education systems
- licensed investigators
- compliance or audit casters
- chapel scholars preparing rites for specific community burdens

### 6.3 Granted Core Model
User receives fixed access to a predefined set of effects through oath, bloodline, devotion, office, pact, divine alignment, or similar authorization.

#### Properties
- stable thematic package
- partial independence from study
- effect behavior strongly linked to role and meaning
- possible obedience obligations
- high theological sensitivity
- failure may be interpreted as moral or covenant breach, not mere miscalculation

#### Typical Setting Contexts
- clergy
- shrine attendants
- oath-bound wardens
- sanctioned protectors
- lineage bearers
- relic house families

### 6.4 Hybrid Model
User has a permanent core set plus selected or prepared auxiliary effects.

#### Properties
- stable identity with adaptive response capacity
- common in institutions where public duties remain fixed but situational demands vary
- useful for academy, guild, or chapel specialists who must retain signature functions while adapting to season, clientele, or threat

### 6.5 Compressed High-Intensity Model
User has very few active energy units, but those units recover more often and are applied at maximum current power density.

#### Properties
- narrow repertoire
- high encounter consistency
- limited breadth
- high short-term impact
- difficult to sustain in protracted ritual administration
- often associated with pacts, elite combat practice, or volatile genius

### 6.6 Site-Gated Model
User cannot meaningfully access certain effects except inside a proper magical environment.

#### Properties
- low portability
- high local precision
- increased safety
- strong dependence on academy halls, chapels, bastions, or domes
- useful in legal systems that want power bound to place

### 6.7 Office-Bound Model
The effect is available not because of personal talent alone, but because the user currently holds the recognized office authorized to perform it.

#### Properties
- legitimacy-sensitive
- role-transferrable
- highly political
- often document-heavy
- may continue through seals, witnesses, and records after the person changes

### 6.8 Anomaly-Conditioned Model
Some users gain access through exposure to dangerous cosmological or contamination conditions.

#### Properties
- unstable
- stigmatized
- difficult to regulate
- high narrative and legal risk
- may be powerful but poorly reproducible
- often feared more than understood

## 7. Execution Requirements

Every magical effect must satisfy execution criteria before activation.

## 7.1 Execution Channels
An effect may require one or more of the following channels.

### 7.1.1 Vocal Channel
The user must produce specific sounds, words, tones, names, cadences, invocations, metrical sequences, weaving songs, doctrinal recitations, or resonance patterns.

#### Functional Purposes
- fix symbolic meaning
- align internal rhythm to external pattern
- stabilize concentration
- announce legal or sacred standing
- synchronize group-casting
- establish witness-worthy ritual form
- reinforce doctrinal legitimacy
- produce acoustic geometry where required

#### Failure Conditions
- silence field
- gagging
- suffocation
- vocal damage
- muting curse
- incompatible transformation
- acoustic distortion chamber
- panic fracture
- inability to pronounce required sacred or technical form correctly
- ritual language mismatch

### 7.1.2 Somatic Channel
The user must perform precise bodily movements, hand forms, stances, directional gestures, needle patterns, loom motions, or seal constructions.

#### Functional Purposes
- localize energy
- define angle and shape
- anchor directionality
- differentiate one effect from a dangerous near-neighbor
- prevent symbolic ambiguity
- interface with material media
- engage body-memory trained by apprenticeship

#### Failure Conditions
- restrained limbs
- paralysis
- insufficient mobility
- occupied hands
- incompatible body morphology
- loss of motor control
- injury to required fingers or joints
- improper spacing relative to ritual circle or apparatus
- panic-induced gesture collapse

### 7.1.3 Material Channel
The effect requires physical media.

#### Material Types
- symbolic common materials
- focus-compatible materials
- specific non-fungible materials
- expensive materials
- consumed catalyst materials
- relic fragments
- blessed fibers
- metals keyed to ward structure
- inks for archival and ritual use
- glass matrices
- shard-sensitive substances
- contaminated containment materials
- living or spiritually linked materials

#### Material Functions
- anchor meaning
- absorb excess volatility
- narrow the effect scope
- provide lawful reproducibility
- satisfy devotional requirements
- prevent collapse into raw uncontrolled release
- bridge between world layers
- bind the effect to object, person, or site

#### Failure Conditions
- material unavailable
- wrong material class
- counterfeit or degraded material
- consumed material already spent
- doctrinally invalid substitute
- impurity
- contaminated channel residue
- local law forbidding possession or use
- material resonance incompatible with region or shrine

### 7.1.4 Spatial Channel
Some effects require a correct positional relation between:

- caster and target
- caster and cardinal or cosmological orientation
- ritual circle and threshold
- witness and subject
- subject and sanctified surface
- containment chamber and external environment

### 7.1.5 Witness Channel
Certain rites require another recognized person to:

- hear
- witness
- certify
- record
- seal
- countersign
- validate identity
- prevent abuse of hidden procedure

This is common in high-risk, legal, liturgical, and inheritance-relevant magic.

## 8. Timing and Activation Modes

## 8.1 Activation Categories

### 8.1.1 Instant Action
Effect may be initiated immediately within active engagement conditions.

#### Typical Use Cases
- defense
- strike effects
- emergency healing
- rapid wards
- countermeasures
- compact utility response

### 8.1.2 Secondary Action
Effect may be executed as a minor but limited-time operation.

#### Operational Meaning
These are effects whose symbolic pattern is compact enough to ride within a narrow execution window but still large enough to matter.

#### Restriction
Secondary-action casting cannot be combined freely with other major effect activations in the same execution window unless explicitly allowed by system rules or site augmentation.

#### Why Restricted
Without restriction, trained users could produce unsafe compression, overwhelming tempo advantage, or unmanageable stacking of field, defense, and offense.

### 8.1.3 Reaction Trigger
Effect activates in response to a qualifying event.

#### Trigger Examples
- incoming attack
- observed hostile magic
- damage taken
- ward contact
- target movement
- oath breach
- threshold crossing
- artifact destabilization
- contamination spike
- spoken name invocation
- false seal detected

#### Tactical Value
Reaction-based magic is the system’s principal interrupt architecture.

### 8.1.4 Extended Ritual
Effect requires sustained uninterrupted execution across minutes, hours, or longer.

#### Properties
- poor combat usability
- improved non-combat precision
- often lower energy waste per unit effect
- supports large-scale changes
- easier to audit afterward
- stronger institutional involvement
- increased interruption risk
- higher importance of correct sequence

#### Typical Use Cases
- consecration
- contamination sealing
- relic attunement
- inheritance recognition
- archive unlocking
- ward installation
- civic blessing
- structural enchantment
- resurrection-class procedure
- long-distance correspondence rites
- legal truth rites
- weather or zone interventions

### 8.1.5 Seasonal or Event-Gated Activation
Some effects can only be performed:

- during specific celestial alignments
- during axial transition windows
- under elemental planetary prominence
- when a shrine opens its renewal cycle
- when institutional calendars permit access
- during crisis declarations
- when a sealed chamber is brought online

## 9. Ritual Subsystem

## 9.1 Ritual Eligibility
Certain effects are flagged as ritual-compatible.

Ritual compatibility means the effect can be externalized from personal reserve into **sequence, symbol, environment, and witness structure**.

## 9.2 Ritual Conversion
A ritual-compatible effect may be executed without expenditure of major personal energy resources if:

- casting time is significantly increased
- ritual conditions are maintained
- sequence remains unbroken
- required witnesses or records are present where needed
- the user has valid ritual access
- the site remains stable
- no contamination event invalidates the chamber
- local authority does not intervene

## 9.3 Ritual Function
This subsystem exists to preserve non-combat magical utility without depleting primary combat resources and to allow communities, chapels, academies, noble houses, and state structures to perform predictable non-improvised magic.

## 9.4 Ritual Components
A ritual may include:

- circles
- threshold markers
- cloth layouts
- needlework patterns
- inks
- names
- hymns
- bells
- controlled fire
- water basins
- mirrors
- shard-safe glass
- seal texts
- relic placement
- rank order of speakers
- timing calls
- civic or doctrinal formulae

## 9.5 Ritual Failure Modes
A ritual is vulnerable to:

- interruption
- sequence drift
- unauthorized participant entry
- contaminated materials
- mistaken identity
- wrong witness order
- false text variant
- broken chamber symmetry
- acoustic contamination
- competing jurisdictional claim
- astral or ethereal bleed
- suppression action by authorities

## 9.6 Ritual Output Types
Rituals are preferred for:

- stable blessing
- curse removal
- community renewal
- legitimacy transfer
- archive unlocking
- site sanctification
- house oath reaffirmation
- protective dome maintenance
- long-form healing
- relic strategy support
- anti-contamination cycling

## 11. Resolution Framework

An effect resolves through one of the following methods.

### 11.1 Automatic Resolution
The effect applies if activation succeeds and the target is valid.

#### Typical Use Cases
- self-buffs
- utility effects
- object creation
- cleaning and preservation
- light generation
- approved ritual sealing
- non-opposed mobility
- authorized archive opening
- lawful sanctification steps
- cooperative healing

#### Design Note
Automatic does not mean risk-free. It means the risk lies before or after resolution rather than in the moment of effect contest.

### 11.2 Offensive Accuracy Resolution
The user makes an attack-style check against target defense.

#### Inputs
- caster proficiency
- casting attribute
- symbolic precision
- situational modifiers
- target defensive value
- environmental clarity
- line stability
- interference pressure

#### Common Uses
- directed force
- projectiles of shaped energy
- targeted debilitation
- precise dispel application
- sharp curse insertion
- single-target anti-relic pulses

### 11.3 Resistance Save Resolution
The target makes a saving or resistance check against the effect difficulty.

#### Inputs
- target resistance statistic
- target situational modifiers
- effect difficulty value
- target doctrinal or spiritual alignment
- contamination tolerance
- ward support
- site assistance
- emotional or cognitive stability
- environmental distortion

#### Result Types
- full negation
- partial reduction
- degraded effect
- delayed onset
- redirected expression
- trace-only manifestation
- full effect application

### 11.4 Staged Resolution
The effect may include multiple steps, such as:

- initial save then ongoing saves
- attack roll then condition application
- partial effect on successful save
- repeated checks to end effect
- area-entry trigger plus turn-start trigger
- ritual phase checks
- seal-recognition stage followed by effect authorization
- layered ward stripping before core effect can land

### 11.5 Legitimacy-Conditioned Resolution
Some magical procedures succeed differently depending on whether the world, site, or institution “recognizes” the user’s right to perform them.

Possible outcomes:
- full effect with authority reinforcement
- reduced effect due to contested standing
- functional effect but legal trace left behind
- ritual refusal
- visible misalignment
- sanctity backlash
- chamber rejection

### 11.6 World-Pressure Conditioned Resolution
Some regions under special stress, such as contamination zones, doom-scar proximities, or storm-influenced territories, may impose resolution modifiers that ordinary spell theory would not predict.

## 12. Offensive Math Model

## 12.1 Magical Attack Value
Used when an effect requires active targeting.

### Formula
Magical Attack Value = proficiency factor + casting attribute modifier + symbolic precision + situational adjustments + local stability bonus or penalty

### Additional Modifiers
- ritual support
- relic focus enhancement
- environmental permissiveness
- visibility condition
- target familiarity
- contamination distortion
- anti-magic drag
- stress penalties
- legal or doctrinal amplification inside sanctioned sites

## 12.2 Magical Difficulty Value
Used when a target must resist an effect.

### Formula
Effect Difficulty = base constant + proficiency factor + casting attribute modifier + symbolic integrity + situational adjustments

### Common Difficulty Enhancers
- higher-tier resource input
- true-name access
- prepared chamber
- house seal or office sanction
- multiple synchronized participants
- target ignorance
- environmental concordance
- doctrinal superiority in sacred space

## 12.3 Bounded Accuracy Principle
Values are intentionally limited within a narrow band to preserve:

- relevance of lower-tier effects
- viability of resistance
- value of preparation
- tactical influence of positioning and conditions
- role of law, architecture, and timing
- usefulness of anti-magic engineering
- importance of trained specialists over raw escalation alone

## 12.4 Precision vs Force
In this setting, stronger magic is not always the same as more damaging magic. An effect can be high-tier because it is:

- hard to authorize
- hard to anchor
- hard to reverse
- socially explosive
- cosmologically risky
- uniquely precise
- capable of changing legitimacy or sanctity

## 14. Persistence Model

## 14.1 Duration Classes

### 14.1.1 Instantaneous
Effect occurs and ends immediately.

#### Examples
- burst force
- flash concealment break
- acute heal pulse
- sealing mark
- destruction of unstable manifestation

### 14.1.2 Fixed Duration
Effect persists for a specified time without maintenance.

#### Examples
- temporary blessing
- garment or tool enhancement
- ward window
- suppression shell
- controlled visual veil
- portable anti-contamination coating

### 14.1.3 Maintained Duration
Effect persists only while the user sustains it.

#### Examples
- active invisibility maintenance
- moving barrier
- controlled summoning
- ongoing mental control
- live dispel contest
- dynamic threat sense
- storm shaping
- active thermal control in dangerous environments

### 14.1.4 Triggered Persistence
Effect remains dormant until activated by a triggering condition.

#### Examples
- threshold wards
- hidden curses
- archive protections
- emergency alarms
- relic fail-safes
- chamber lockdowns
- shrine transgression responses

### 14.1.5 Permanent
Effect does not expire naturally.

#### Examples
- relic-level enchantment
- inherited sanctification
- irreversible contamination
- fixed architectural ward lattice
- lasting divine mark
- major world-scar consequences
- certain memory injuries or restorations

## 14.2 Persistence Burden
Longer persistence usually requires one or more of:

- higher-tier resource expenditure
- maintained concentration
- periodic ritual renewals
- supporting anchors
- site support
- communal upkeep
- legal records
- cleansing cycles
- material replacement
- priestly or scholarly review

## 14.3 Persistence Trace
The longer an effect lasts, the more likely it leaves:

- signature residue
- legal discoverability
- spiritual imprint
- material wear
- local habituation
- social dependency
- institutional maintenance cost

## 15. Concentration and Maintenance

## 15.1 Single-Maintenance Rule
A user may actively maintain only one major sustained effect at a time unless an explicit exception exists through specialization, relic augmentation, linked casters, or architectural load transfer.

## 15.2 Purpose
This prevents abusive layering of simultaneous control, defense, summoning, concealment, and field effects, while preserving the importance of choice, vulnerability, and tactical disruption.

## 15.3 Maintenance Failure Conditions
A maintained effect may end if:

- the user voluntarily ends it
- the user is incapacitated
- the user loses required focus
- the user fails a disruption check after damage or shock
- another maintained effect is initiated
- a suppression field interferes
- a chamber revokes local support
- a witness line is broken for rites that require it
- contamination or astral pressure destabilizes the channel
- the ritual geometry is physically damaged

## 15.4 Tactical Implications
This rule makes sustained magic a commitment and creates value for:

- bodyguarding the caster
- isolating the maintainer
- suppressing sight lines
- forcing movement
- applying procedural interruption
- targeting hands or voice
- using anti-magic architecture
- breaking ritual circles
- drawing casters into environments that disfavor their effect profile

## 15.5 Institutional Maintenance Transfer
Certain institutions can offload part of the maintenance cost from person to place. This is one reason academies, chapels, bastions, and domes matter. They are not only locations; they are **maintenance-capable magical machines**.

- access to a compatible interference method

### Possible Outcomes
- full cancellation

- contest-based interruption
- partial destabilization
- redirected effect
- delayed resolution
- failed interruption

### Countercasting Methods

- doctrinal contradiction
- acoustic break
- focus disruption
- anti-resonance discharge

## 19. Interruption and Failure States

## 19.1 Failure Classes

### 19.1.1 Validation Failure
Effect cannot begin because prerequisites are not met.

### 19.1.2 Resource Failure
Effect cannot begin because cost cannot be paid.

### 19.1.3 Authorization Failure
Effect cannot begin or cannot hold because the user lacks required legitimacy, office, sanctity, or legal standing.

### 19.1.4 Execution Failure
Effect is interrupted during activation.

### 19.1.5 Resolution Failure
Effect activates but is resisted, negated, misses, or lands in degraded form.

### 19.1.6 Stability Failure
Effect manifests incorrectly due to overload, corruption, hostile environment, broken geometry, or breach of structure.

### 19.1.7 Trace Failure
The effect works, but leaves behind actionable evidence, residue, witness exposure, or reputational consequence severe enough to count as practical failure.

### 19.1.8 Sanctity Failure
A rite resolves mechanically but is judged spiritually invalid, polluted, or blasphemous, with long-tail religious or communal consequences.

- ritual sequence drift
- wrong chamber
- wrong season
- contamination intrusion

- local law conflict

## 20. Power Scaling Across Advancement Bands

## 20.1 Early Band
Magic is constrained, personal, and situational.

#### Characteristics
- limited energy
- narrow effect catalog
- dependence on tools and place
- high value of correct form
- visible risk from misuse
- local social consequence matters more than raw output

#### World Feel
At this band, magic feels intimate, artisanal, or frighteningly fragile. A person can improve a room, protect a threshold, mend an injury, or make a fight unfair, but not yet redefine regional order.

## 20.2 Mid Band
Magic becomes a major force multiplier.

#### Characteristics
- improved control options
- larger areas
- stronger restoration
- tactical mobility
- battlefield shaping
- professional anti-magic interaction
- increased legal visibility
- stronger dependency on institutions and records

#### World Feel
At this band, mages, ritualists, clerics, enchanters, and relic operators begin to matter to policy, war, trade, and class structure.

## 20.3 High Band
Magic becomes strategic and civilizational.

#### Characteristics
- long-range transit
- resurrection-class operations
- large-scale summoning
- permanent architecture enchantment
- anti-contamination systems
- site sanctification on institutional scale
- relic network use
- historical memory intervention
- world-scar management
- major political destabilization potential

#### World Feel
At this band, magic becomes inseparable from who governs, who remembers, who travels safely, who owns relics, who can restore disaster, and who can survive contact with the world’s deeper fractures.

## 20.4 Mythic or World-Structural Band
Rare effects interact with cosmology itself.

#### Characteristics
- direct use of divine residue or shard logic
- manipulation of planar seams
- interaction with doom-scar conditions
- large-scale weather or polarity consequences
- fate of houses, shrines, bastions, or continents
- destabilization or restoration of long-held balances

## 22. Balancing Constraints

The system remains stable only if the following constraints are enforced:

- major effects require finite resources
- action economy restricts execution volume
- maintained effects are limited
- execution channels can be disrupted
- site and law can forbid or alter performance
- targets can resist
- access breadth is limited by preparation, known-list constraints, or institutional control
- expensive effects require expensive materials
- countermeasures exist
- recovery pacing prevents unlimited peak output
- contaminated, unstable, or high-pressure regions impose additional risk
- social legitimacy affects practical viability
- records and witnesses can create consequence even after success

## 22.1 Soft Balancing Factors
Not all balancing is numeric. Important soft constraints include:

- fear of prosecution
- doctrinal scrutiny
- tariff burden
- rarity of trained mentors
- relic scarcity
- risk of losing guild or chapel standing
- contamination stigma
- suspicion triggers in procedure-heavy institutions
- public memory of magical disasters
- noble or state monopolization

## 22.2 Hard Balancing Factors
Hard constraints include:

- anti-magic architecture
- limited resource tiers
- concentration limits
- chamber requirements
- seal and witness rules
- relic compatibility thresholds
- contamination failure
- legal prohibition backed by force
- environmental dead zones
- ritual interruption vulnerability

## 23. Design Summary

### 23.1 Core Definition
Magic is a finite, structured, reality-altering system governed by:

- cosmological substrate
- access rights
- authorization and legitimacy
- energy expenditure
- execution requirements
- targeting legality
- resistance mechanics
- duration rules
- maintenance limitations
- material and ritual channels
- interference systems
- law, sanctity, and institutional response

### 23.2 System Identity
This is a **high-control, setting-embedded, jurisdiction-sensitive, resource-driven magical architecture** optimized for:

- tactical depth
- strategic planning
- regional variation
- institutional power expression
- meaningful tradeoffs
- strong worldbuilding consequences
- interaction between metaphysics and governance
- escalation from personal practice to civilizational infrastructure

### 23.3 Final Technical Summary
Magic is a rules-governed method for imposing non-natural outcomes onto reality through constrained expenditure of energy, symbol, environment, and authority, with all effects subject to activation requirements, legal and sacred context, target validation, resistance, maintenance limits, contamination and cosmological pressure, and disruption by opposing magical, institutional, or political forces.

### 23.4 Setting-Specific Summary
On this world, magic is not merely what a gifted person can do. It is what a broken but stabilized cosmos still permits, what divine residue still remembers, what institutions have learned to codify, what laws try to contain, what relics preserve, what chapels sanctify, what academies formalize, what states monopolize, and what anomalies threaten to unmake.

### 23.5 Use Standard
When writing, adjudicating, or expanding magic for this setting, every new effect should be evaluated against the following questions:

- What cosmological layer or substrate does it rely on?
- Who is allowed to use it?
- Who claims authority over it?
- What resources does it consume?
- What channels does it require?
- What makes it legal, sacred, illicit, or contaminated?
- What site or region changes its behavior?
- What countermeasure can answer it?
- What residue, witness, or trace does it leave?
- What part of the world becomes different if it succeeds repeatedly?
