# Ironhold Quarry — Text RPG Flavor Packet

**Parent polity:** Principality of Roaz (House Roaz)
**Type:** Industrial extraction site
**Truth layer:** direct_canon

## Atmosphere

The quarry face is the first thing that registers — a pale, stepped wall cut down into the earth in straight lines, with haul-ramps spiraling against the stone and the ore cars moving on them in both directions all day. Dust coats everything at the rim: the guardhouse shutters, the labor shed roofs, the boots of anyone who has stood still for more than a minute. The sound is continuous and unmusical. Pick strikes, cart wheels on rails, a foreman's whistle cutting through it, and under all of that the low grinding of the ore chutes feeding the transport yard below.

Roazian order here is not ceremonial. It is the straight line of the face, the exact placement of the labor zones, the guards at the rail heads, and the papers a person is expected to produce when asked. Nothing about this place is negotiable. That is the point.

## Scene Openers

1. **First bell, rim guardhouse.** A patrol checks papers at the intake lane. Carts are lined four deep; a driver ahead is being asked twice about a seal, and the ore officer's pen is already moving. Dust lifts with every gust off the face. The line does not move until the officer decides it moves.
2. **Midday, labor shed run-out.** A shift has come up from the lower cuts. They file past the water barrels in silence, the foremen counting heads as they pass, and nobody drinks until they are cleared of the counting line. A miner with a blood-blackened hand is being looked at quickly by the shed physician; she is not asking for help so much as waiting for permission to stop.
3. **Shift change, transport yard.** Ore cars are being weighed at the scale. A quarry overseer is arguing with a haulage contractor about a manifest; the number on the tag does not match the number on the scale, and neither man wants the officer involved. A runner has already been sent, but he is walking slow.
4. **Night watch, upper terrace.** Lamps are lit along the guardrail — not festive, functional. A pair of guards are walking the rim, and their boots make the only regular sound above the rock. Somewhere below, a last crew is still working a cut by lantern, because the overseer decided the day's yield was short.

## Social Register

- Speak when spoken to. Volunteered opinions at the face are read as either ignorance or trouble.
- Address an ore officer by role — "officer" will do — and produce papers before asked if you can see the officer wants them.
- Miners and drovers speak in short sentences about weight, distance, and weather. Anything longer sounds like a complaint, and complaints are filed in writing or not at all.
- Do not gesture with tools. Do not stand between a loaded cart and the scale. Do not step into a labor zone without a foreman's nod, not even to speak to somebody inside it.
- "Contraband" is a word that ends conversations. Using it casually, even as a joke, gets the speaker escorted off the rim.

## Signature Details

- **The quarry face itself.** Cut in disciplined steps, each bench roughly the same depth, each face showing the same pale stone. The walls are painted with tar lines marking labor-zone boundaries that guards enforce without discussion. A visitor who crosses a tar line has crossed into a different argument.
- **The chalked manifest shed.** A low stone building at the transport yard where every cart's intake and outflow is logged. Clerks sit two to a bench, and a manifest error logged there follows a driver for years. Drivers queue to watch their own entries being made because they have learned not to trust a copy.
- **The guard's nod.** It is the only informal currency here. A guard who has seen you enter and leave a dozen times without incident will nod once at the gate, and that nod is what makes the fifteenth time faster than the first. Outsiders do not receive nods; they receive procedure.

## Complication Seed

A shift returned from a lower cut with one fewer miner than went down, and the foreman's head-count log reads correct — which means either the log was altered, or the miner never entered the count to begin with. An ore officer has taken the log for review, and the shed physician has been told not to discuss her afternoon. A senior quarry overseer is quietly looking for someone who does not work for House Roaz, does not carry Union papers, and does not have a stake in which answer comes out — someone who can walk the cut and the log together and tell her which one was changed. The window is before the officer escalates up the hill.
