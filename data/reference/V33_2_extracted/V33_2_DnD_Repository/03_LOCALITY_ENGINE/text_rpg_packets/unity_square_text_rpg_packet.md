# Unity Square — Text RPG Flavor Packet

**Parent polity:** The Union
**Type:** Urban exchange quarter
**Truth layer:** direct_canon

## Atmosphere

Unity Square is a stone-floored plaza the size of a small district, with the exchange arcades built into the inner walls and the inspection stalls standing in orderly rows across the center. Negotiated trade happens here instead of inherited claims, and the plaza is arranged so that every deal can be witnessed — arcade benches face the center, the inspection stalls face outward, and the clerks' rostrum sits on a raised step at the north end where everyone can see the day's postings.

The sound is a blended hum of bargaining, seal-stamps, and the metallic click of weight-sets being checked against certified counters. Paper moves constantly: draft contracts, inspection tickets, spot-trade chits. People who do business here walk with a folder or a slim case and hand things across a counter rather than shoulder to shoulder. The Square teaches a register that is polite, transactional, and public — status is what you can negotiate it to be on a given day.

## Scene Openers

1. **Mid-morning, inspection row.** A merchant is arguing the grade of a bolt of cloth with an inspector, quietly and without raising the voice that matters. A clerk at the rostrum is already moving to post the dispute number on the board; if the matter is not settled before the board's next refresh, it is public. The merchant knows this. So does the inspector.
2. **Midday, arcade bench.** Two brokers are sharing a bench and a small pot of tea. They are not looking at each other. They are both watching the inspection row and trading sentences about a third broker, who has been in and out of the stalls three times this morning with the same folder. Whatever the folder holds, neither wants to be seen asking about it.
3. **Late afternoon, holding room door.** A porter has been called in to remove a broken crate. The holding room at the edge of the plaza — the safeZone — is where deals that went loud get resolved out of sight, and it is being used now. The door is ajar. A mediator is inside, and two parties are visible through the gap, each waiting to be called in separately.
4. **Evening, board refresh.** The clerk climbs the rostrum step to update the posting board for the overnight cycle. A small crowd gathers without apparent purpose. What they are reading is who filed what today, who settled, who escalated, and whose dispute number is now going to sit on the board overnight in full public view.

## Social Register

- State terms clearly and early. Vagueness in a first exchange here reads as either a lack of preparation or a setup.
- Inspectors are addressed by badge number when known, by role otherwise. Do not use a surname on the floor unless the inspector offers it first.
- The posting board is respected. Pointing at it is fine. Laughing at an entry on it is not — the person named is probably within thirty paces and is expected to be carrying themselves with dignity until the matter is closed.
- Negotiated status is real status. A trader who has talked their way to a preferred stall has earned it for the day; no one litigates the stall back on grounds of family or prior claim. That is the whole promise of the Square.
- Public humiliation is the punishable misstep. A deal can go badly, loudly, and on the record; shaming another party beyond the facts of the deal is what the mediators will intervene on.

## Signature Details

- **The posting board.** A long dark board at the foot of the rostrum, rewritten three times a day by a clerk with a practiced hand. Dispute numbers, settlement notices, inspection pending, and a short column for Union notices that the Guild Council wants publicly visible. Locals read it the way other towns read a newspaper.
- **The certified weight-sets.** Brass-cased, sealed, kept in locked cabinets at each inspection stall. Each cabinet has a ring-seal from the Guild Council and a check-mark log posted on the side. A broken seal is not something anyone tries to bluff.
- **The holding room door.** Plain, heavy, set in the east arcade. The door itself is unremarkable; what is noted is who goes in, in what order, and how long between the first party's entry and the second's. Seasoned traders watch the door more closely than they watch the board.

## Complication Seed

An inspection ticket has been filed on a cloth shipment whose grade is not actually in dispute — the grade is fine. The ticket exists because the filing broker needed a dispute number on the board to block a rival trade for the day, and the cloth was the only goods he had a plausible grievance with. The inspector knows the filing is false but cannot say so on the board without either naming the broker or admitting the Square's process was used as a stall tactic. The inspector is looking for an outside party who can make the true trade — the one the false filing is blocking — move through on a different path before the board refresh, quietly enough that the filing broker loses his leverage without the Square having to admit the leverage ever existed. The window is the afternoon.
