# Sheresh — Text RPG Flavor Packet

**Parent polity:** Sheresh Communes
**Type:** Specialist coastal commune node
**Truth layer:** conservative_inference (zero canon NPCs — all figures below are generic roles, not named individuals)

## Atmosphere

Sheresh sits against the cold edge of the sea, where the land runs out in low black rock and the water does not warm through the year. The commune buildings are squat and windowed for light rather than view, the walls thickened against wind off the water. Smoke from the seal-oil lamps gives the lower rooms a close, resinous smell that clings to cloth. Outside, the sound is the sea and the wind and the clack of drying frames being turned on the racks along the shore.

Life here is cold-country practical. Nothing is wasted and nothing is decorative for its own sake. A tool hung on a wall is hung because it will be used again tomorrow. A rite spoken at a meal is spoken because the speaker means it; flourish in a commune rite reads as an outsider affectation. People work close to each other all day and do not need to fill the air between them with talk.

## Scene Openers

1. **First light, drying racks on the shore.** The morning crew is turning the frames — fish, kelp, strips of net for repair. Their hands are bare despite the cold because gloves catch on the frame ties. A junior member of the crew is timing the work against a small weighted string hung from a rack post, and the elder beside her has not looked at the string once but corrects her rhythm twice in the first ten minutes.
2. **Midmorning, the commune hall.** A meeting is in progress — a dozen people at a long table, a ledger open in the middle, a pot of something hot on the hearth behind them. They are discussing a repair rotation, or a supply allocation, or a dispute; the subject does not change the shape of the meeting. Everyone speaks in turn, nobody interrupts, and when the ledger is written the write is read aloud before the meeting closes.
3. **Afternoon, cold-water basin.** A small crew is washing tools and equipment in the outdoor basin fed by the pump at the wall. The water is cold enough that the work is done in short bursts, each person stepping back to warm hands at a brazier between turns. An outsider who tried to stay at the basin would be noted and gently replaced before their hands stopped working.
4. **Evening, seal-oil lamp light.** The common room fills slowly as the work day ends. People eat at a long bench, not a table; the bench is shared with anyone who is in the room. Conversation is low. A storyteller has begun something about a storm three winters back that the younger listeners have not heard, and the elders listening already know where the storytelling pauses will fall.

## Social Register

- Speech is plain and low. Voices raised in the common room are for emergencies; voices raised elsewhere are for the sea. A shouted greeting on arrival reads as bluster.
- Do not refuse food offered at a bench. Eat what is set down, in the portion set down. Leaving food is a stronger statement than any commune member would make aloud.
- Work is earned, not asked for. A visitor who wants to help with a task stands near it without being in the way until someone hands them a tool. Asking to help directly is acceptable but clumsy.
- Address commune members by role where role is visible — "rack-warden," "hall-keeper," "basin-turn" — and do not ask for names until a second meeting. Names are given, not inquired after.
- Do not speak ill of the sea. It is not superstition. It is a working habit among people whose livelihood depends on not assuming the water is kind.

## Signature Details

- **The drying racks.** Long wooden frames set in rows along the shore above the highwater line, each rack with a crew assigned and a turning schedule timed by the weighted strings hung from the posts. The strings are replaced every season; the rhythm they mark is older than any living member of the commune.
- **The seal-oil lamps.** Small, stoppered, burned at low flame in every interior room after sundown. The smell is characteristic and carries on cloth. A Sheresh member visiting another commune is recognizable half a room away by the smell alone.
- **The ledger-and-read.** Every commune meeting closes with the scribe reading the day's ledger entry aloud before the meeting disperses. Members are expected to listen. A correction raised at the read is settled immediately; a correction raised after the read is raised at the next meeting, because the read is where the record becomes the record.

## Complication Seed

A drying-rack crew has logged a lower-than-usual yield two turns in a row, and the rack-warden cannot account for the difference from wind, tide, or catch — the catch has been normal, the weather unremarkable. She suspects small theft from the racks overnight, but the commune has not had theft of that kind in her memory and naming it aloud at a meeting would shape the commune around it before she is ready. She is looking for a visitor who is not of the commune, does not owe the commune, and does not owe anyone nearby — someone who can watch the racks for a night or two from a distance and tell her what is actually happening before she has to say anything in the hall. The window is the next meeting, three days out.
