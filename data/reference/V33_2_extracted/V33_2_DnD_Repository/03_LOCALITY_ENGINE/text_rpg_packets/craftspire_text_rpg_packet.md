# Craftspire — Text RPG Flavor Packet

**Parent polity:** The Union (Principalities, Union Successor Heartland)
**Type:** Urbanized production district
**Truth layer:** direct_canon

## Atmosphere

Craftspire is stacked vertical. Workhouses and copy towers crowd the air above the lanes, and the lanes themselves are narrow because the carts come first — materials in, finished work out, always on a schedule somebody's ledger is counting. The smell depends on the block: hot wax and vellum near the copy towers, oil and filings around the tool yards, dye steam at the textile stretches. People walk with their shoulders turned, because the queue court matters and nobody cuts it twice.

Prestige here is not inherited. It is the visible fact of skilled output, licensed and witnessed, with a guild mark stamped somewhere a customer can see. A workshop's reputation hangs on whether commissioned work leaves the premises on time and uncopied. Loss of either is a ruin nobody pretends is small.

## Scene Openers

1. **Mid-morning, copy tower district.** A binder's apprentice is stacking quires on the balcony rail. A copyist two floors up calls down a page number, and a line of people at the street-level queue court pretends not to count how many ahead of them. Someone near the front has a commission slip whose wax seal is broken, and two clerks are arguing in low voices about whether to honor it.
2. **Late afternoon, materials yard.** A cart has come in short — one crate of fixative missing from a three-crate manifest. The yard foreman is writing a correction in chalk on the slate board at the gate, which means the workshops upstream will learn about the shortfall the way they always do: from the chalk, not a runner. A journeyman is already moving to reshuffle her afternoon.
3. **Dusk, between shifts.** The cutters are changing over. Night crew files in with their tool rolls under their arms; day crew files out with hands still grey from the work. The tavern at the corner of the lane has its shutters open early because the opening rite for a book run is tonight, and the senior copyist will expect everyone there to have a hand free for a cup.
4. **Raining, ledger balcony.** A Union inspector is reading a workshop's output book against its materials intake. The workshop's owner is standing at a polite distance with her hands folded into her apron, and the apprentice by the door has been told to fetch water twice already — the third time is what it will mean if things go badly.

## Social Register

- Speech is clipped and specific. Tradespeople name the quire, the grade of paper, the run size — vagueness reads as either ignorance or cover.
- "Commission" is a serious word. Do not say "my book" when you mean "my commission." The ledger cares about the latter.
- Apprentices do not speak first. A journeyman fielding a question on a master's behalf will answer without breaking the rhythm of her hands.
- Compliments about finish are currency. "Clean rule" or "even tone" is a meaningful thing to say about someone else's copy. Empty flattery marks the speaker as outside the trade.
- Do not touch commissioned work. Do not lean on a binding stone. Do not handle anyone's copy paper with ungloved hands near a drying rack.

## Signature Details

- **The copy towers.** Narrow stone stacks, four to six floors, with shuttered balconies at each level and a rope-and-basket system for moving quires up and finished folios down without the stairs. The tops of the towers are where the senior copyists work; the light is best there.
- **The chalk board at every materials gate.** White slate, black frame, chalked in a standardized clerk's hand. Workshops read it first thing in the morning and last thing before close. A mistake on the board is a small institutional event.
- **The opening rite.** Before a major work run — a whole edition, a named commission, a tariffed text — the master copyist sets a cup of something on the workbench corner, names the run aloud, and the crew drinks together. It is not a temple observance. It is a practical devotion in a workshop alcove, and skipping it is the kind of thing the crew remembers when the run goes long.

## Complication Seed

A sheet of a commissioned run has turned up outside the workshop it came from — pasted to a lamppost two streets over, or folded into a market handbill, or simply missing from the bindery's count. The workshop's licensed owner is not telling the Union copying office yet, because the tariff on copy theft is steep and the investigation will freeze her other work. She is looking for someone who is not Union, not a rival workshop, and not a friend of either — someone who can find out how the sheet got out before she has to file a notice. The clock is how long before somebody else files it for her.
