# Districts (Shelkopolis) — Text RPG Flavor Packet

**Parent polity:** Principality of Shelk
**Type:** Urban mixed districts of Shelkopolis
**Truth layer:** direct_canon

## Atmosphere

Shelkopolis's districts read in layers. The upper quarter is pale stone with golden accents, indigo and emerald banners at the gate of every house that cares to be noticed, and Roadwardens in bright plume visible at every turn. The common quarter is busier and lower — workshop fronts, tea-houses, shrines with open doors, the same polished feel as the upper quarter but with more traffic and less space. The low ward takes all of this and compresses it against the service lanes: cart yards, porter barracks, a few shrines that stay lit all night, the streets where most of the day's work actually begins and ends.

Fashion is not decorative here. It is civic. The cut of a cloak, the pattern of a sash, the trim on a tunic — all of it tells the districts' reader where a person stands, where they are going, and whether to make way. Compliments on attire are normal among strangers of equal standing. Missing a compliment owed upward is a misstep. Offering one downward is fine, as long as it is not mistaken for condescension.

## Scene Openers

1. **Morning, upper quarter street.** A Roadwarden patrol passes on the cobbles, plumes catching the light. A household attendant is sweeping the threshold of a noble house; she pauses when the patrol passes and resumes without looking up. A delivery cart is waiting at the service gate because the front gate is not used for deliveries even when the front gate is empty.
2. **Midday, common quarter workshop row.** A tailor's apprentice is running a bolt of cloth across the lane to a dyer's shop. Both proprietors stand in their doorways and watch him run without calling after him; the arrangement is old, the price is already set, and nobody needs to speak. A student from the scholars' district is loitering outside the tailor's window pretending not to window-shop.
3. **Afternoon, low ward lane.** Porters are loading a cart with bolts and cases bound for the upper quarter. The foreman checks each piece against a slip; a Roadwarden is leaning against a post watching the load, not because he suspects anything, but because the cart is bound for a house whose stewards like the wardens' presence visible at intake.
4. **Evening, shrine in the common quarter.** The shrine is small and open-fronted, with a covered basin of water and a rail of woven offerings. Residents stop on their way home, touch the rail, and move on. No words. A Fashion Artisans' Collective banner hangs on the wall of the building next door, freshly cleaned, because the Collective's monthly procession is tomorrow and the wardens want the route tidy.

## Social Register

- Observe fashion. Compliment it when offered to an equal; acknowledge it when shown by a superior. Noticing an inferior's effort is generous; mocking it is an insult the whole street will remember.
- Address a Roadwarden as "warden" without a given name. They will introduce themselves if the situation calls for it. Patrol wardens on route duty will not.
- Guild marks are read without comment. A shopkeeper will see a visitor's guild mark and adjust their conversation accordingly; it is not necessary to explain the mark, and explaining it too eagerly looks like overreach.
- Between quarters, move by the service lanes if you are doing service work and by the main streets if you are not. Being in the wrong lane for your dress is the kind of thing the wardens correct without heat, but they correct it.
- Do not dismiss fashion as superficial. Do not speak loudly about patronage in the open. Do not treat a Fashion Artisans' procession as merely a procession — it is a civic statement the districts take seriously.

## Signature Details

- **The banner houses.** Noble and senior guild houses hang their banners above the main gate and change them on a posted schedule tied to civic events. Reading the banners up a street tells an alert visitor which houses are observant of which current civic observance, which have guests, and which are in a period of mourning or reflection.
- **The plume.** A Roadwarden's plume is dyed to that warden's unit and trimmed to that warden's rank. Seasoned residents read plumes before faces and address wardens accordingly. A disheveled or dust-dulled plume on a warden who should be on parade duty is a small scandal.
- **The guild-mark doorstep.** Many common-quarter workshops have their guild mark inlaid in the threshold stone or painted above the lintel. Apprentices sweep the threshold first thing in the morning and last thing at night. A neglected doorstep reads to the street like a neglected book.

## Complication Seed

A bolt of cloth commissioned for a Fashion Artisans' Collective procession has come back from the dyer's with the wrong sash-pattern woven in — not obviously wrong, but wrong to anyone in the Collective who knows the pattern. The tailor suspects the dyer, who has been courted by a rival house; the dyer suspects the tailor's apprentice, who has been unhappy about the commission's hours. Neither wants to raise the question openly because the procession is tomorrow and the Roadwarden captain has already confirmed the route. The tailor is looking for an outsider who can walk the cloth's trip back — tailor to dyer and dyer to tailor — and determine whether the pattern went in at the loom, at the dye-bath, or somewhere on the road between them. The window is before the cloth is walked in the procession with half the upper quarter watching.
